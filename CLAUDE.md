# Neighborhood Life Intelligence Platform

## Project Status

This document is the product and business source of truth for the project.

**Working title:** Neighborhood Life  
**Primary market:** Turkey  
**Initial launch market:** Ankara  
**Core product question:** “What is daily life like in this neighborhood?”  
**Product type:** Consumer neighborhood intelligence platform with a B2B product for real-estate professionals.

The product must not become another generic business directory or a visual copy of Google Maps. Its purpose is to help people decide whether a neighborhood fits their daily life before they rent or buy a home.

---

## 1. One-Sentence Pitch

Before renting or buying a home, users can enter an address or select a neighborhood and see how easily they can access groceries, pharmacies, restaurants, schools, public transport, gyms, veterinarians, and other daily needs.

---

## 2. Product Vision

Property platforms describe the home but usually provide little useful information about the daily experience outside the home.

A listing may show the number of rooms, square meters, floor, heating system, and price, but it rarely answers questions such as:

- Can I buy groceries without using a car?
- Are there good pharmacies and health services nearby?
- Is the neighborhood suitable for a family with children?
- Are there cafés, restaurants, gyms, and social places nearby?
- Is there a veterinarian within walking distance?
- Are essential businesses open during evenings and weekends?
- Which of two neighborhoods fits my lifestyle better?
- Does the area have many businesses but poor variety?
- Is the available business data reliable and current?

Neighborhood Life turns fragmented place data into a clear, personalized neighborhood report.

The long-term vision is to become the neighborhood intelligence layer used by consumers, real-estate agents, property websites, developers, relocation companies, and property managers in Turkey.

---

## 3. The Problem

### 3.1 Consumer problem

People commonly choose a home using incomplete information. They visit the property once, check the approximate location on a map, and manually search for a few nearby businesses.

This process is slow and inconsistent. Raw map results do not explain the neighborhood as a complete daily-living system.

A map may show dozens of businesses, but the user still has to determine:

- Which ones are actually useful?
- How close are they on foot?
- Are there enough alternatives?
- Are they well reviewed?
- Are essential categories missing?
- Does the neighborhood suit the user’s specific lifestyle?

### 3.2 Real-estate professional problem

Real-estate agents often repeat the same neighborhood explanations for every property. They may say that a listing is “close to everything,” but they rarely have an objective, shareable report to support that claim.

Agents need a tool that can:

- Add neighborhood value to a property listing.
- Give buyers and tenants greater confidence.
- Differentiate the agent from competitors.
- Generate a branded neighborhood report.
- Capture leads from people researching an area.
- Reduce repetitive questions.

### 3.3 Data problem

Business and neighborhood information is distributed across multiple sources. Some sources are incomplete, some are expensive, and some have strict storage and display rules.

The product must combine data carefully instead of promising a perfect or exhaustive database of every business in Turkey.

---

## 4. The Solution

The application creates an understandable report for an address or neighborhood.

Each report combines:

- Nearby business categories.
- Distance from the selected address or neighborhood center.
- Walking accessibility.
- Category variety.
- Business density.
- Operating-hour coverage.
- Ratings and review volume where legally available.
- Public transport access where reliable data exists.
- User-selected lifestyle priorities.
- Resident comments submitted directly to the platform.
- A confidence score showing the quality and completeness of the underlying data.

The main output is not a list of businesses. The main output is an answer to:

> “Would my daily life work well in this neighborhood?”

## 8. Core Differentiation

Google Maps answers:

> “Where is a pharmacy?”

Neighborhood Life answers:

> “How well does this neighborhood cover my healthcare and daily-needs requirements compared with another neighborhood?”

The main differentiators are:

1. **Neighborhood-first analysis**  
   Businesses are evidence used to evaluate a neighborhood. They are not the final product.

2. **Personalized scoring**  
   A family, student, pet owner, and car-free professional should receive different results.

3. **Walking-access focus**  
   Raw distance is less useful than practical access within a selected time or radius.

4. **Category coverage and missing-service analysis**  
   The application explains what the neighborhood lacks.

5. **Neighborhood comparison**  
   Users can compare two or three areas using the same scoring model.

6. **Real-estate context**  
   Reports can be connected to a specific property address or listing.

7. **Transparent scoring**  
   Users can see why a score is high or low.

8. **Data confidence**  
   Every report includes a confidence indicator based on data coverage and freshness.

9. **First-party resident knowledge**  
   The platform gradually builds its own neighborhood and business comments instead of depending permanently on third-party reviews.

## 11. Scoring Model

### 11.1 Main scores

Each report should contain:

- **Neighborhood Life Score:** 0–100.
- **Category Scores:** 0–100.
- **Lifestyle Fit Scores:** 0–100.
- **Data Confidence Score:** 0–100.

### 11.2 Default category weights

Suggested initial weights:

| Category | Default Weight |
|---|---:|
| Daily essentials | 20% |
| Health and wellbeing | 15% |
| Transport and mobility | 15% |
| Family and education | 10% |
| Food and social life | 10% |
| Fitness and recreation | 10% |
| Business quality and reliability | 10% |
| Pet services | 5% |
| Late-hour convenience | 5% |

Weights must be stored in configuration, not hard-coded into application logic.

### 11.3 Category score components

Each category score can combine:

1. **Coverage**  
   Whether essential subcategories exist within the selected area.

2. **Proximity**  
   How close useful places are to the selected address or neighborhood center.

3. **Choice**  
   Whether the user has alternatives instead of only one provider.

4. **Diversity**  
   Whether the available places cover different subcategories.

5. **Quality**  
   Ratings and review volume where legally available.

6. **Availability**  
   Operating-hour coverage, evenings, and weekends.

7. **Accessibility**  
   Practical walking or transport access.

8. **Freshness and confidence**  
   The age, coverage, and reliability of the source data.

### 11.4 Important scoring rules

- Do not reward raw business count without limits. Ten supermarkets should not automatically make a neighborhood twice as good as five.
- Use diminishing returns after useful category coverage is achieved.
- Missing essential categories should produce a clear penalty.
- Use Bayesian or confidence-adjusted ratings rather than simple average ratings.
- A business with a 5.0 rating from two reviews should not automatically outrank a business with a 4.7 rating from hundreds of reviews.
- Duplicate businesses must be merged before scoring.
- Closed or inactive businesses must be excluded when reliable status exists.
- A low-confidence report must clearly say so.
- The score must be explainable in plain language.
- Sponsored businesses must never affect neighborhood scores.

### 11.5 Example explanation

> Daily essentials scored 84/100 because the address has several grocery options, two bakeries, and a pharmacy within approximately ten minutes. The score is reduced because there is limited late-evening coverage and only one produce shop nearby.

## 12. Data Strategy

The product should use a multi-source model.

### 12.1 OpenStreetMap as the baseline

Use OpenStreetMap data as the foundational place and geographic dataset.

For an initial prototype, use Overpass queries for a limited number of neighborhoods.

For a production system or national expansion:

- Download the Turkey OpenStreetMap extract.
- Import relevant points, polygons, roads, and boundaries into PostGIS.
- Normalize OSM tags into the platform taxonomy.
- Refresh the data on a scheduled basis.
- Keep source identifiers and source timestamps.
- Respect OpenStreetMap attribution and licensing requirements.

Do not depend on the public OpenStreetMap tile server for production-scale traffic. Use a commercial tile provider or host appropriate tiles.

### 12.2 Neighborhood boundaries

Neighborhood boundaries are a critical dependency.

Possible sources:

- OpenStreetMap administrative boundaries.
- Municipality open-data portals.
- Licensed geographic datasets.
- Manually reviewed polygons for the MVP.

Every neighborhood boundary should have:

- Source.
- Source identifier.
- Last update date.
- Confidence level.
- Manual-review status.

If an official or reliable polygon is not available, the product can temporarily use a clearly labeled approximate area. It must not silently present an approximate boundary as official.

### 12.3 Google Places enrichment

Google Places may be used for on-demand enrichment such as:

- Google Place ID.
- Current rating.
- User rating count.
- Operating hours.
- Business status.
- Website.
- Phone number.
- A limited number of review excerpts.
- Google Maps link.

Important product constraints:

- Do not scrape Google Maps.
- Do not design the product around downloading every Google review.
- The Places API returns a limited number of reviews for a place.
- Google content has caching, storage, display, and attribution rules.
- Store Google Place IDs where permitted, but treat other Google content as licensed, refreshable enrichment.
- Keep Google-derived fields isolated from the permanent open-data record.
- Verify current Google Maps Platform terms before implementation and before every major data-model change.
- If Google Places results are displayed on a map, use a map implementation permitted by Google’s current policies.
- If an open MapLibre map is used, Google-derived enrichment should be presented in a separately attributed experience unless current legal review confirms a broader implementation is permitted.

### 12.4 First-party data

The long-term data advantage should come from the platform’s own users and partners.

First-party data can include:

- Resident neighborhood reviews.
- User-confirmed business corrections.
- Reports of closed businesses.
- New business submissions.
- Evening noise comments.
- Parking experience.
- Walkability observations.
- Family suitability.
- Pet friendliness.
- Agent-verified neighborhood notes.
- User-created lists.

First-party reviews must be moderated and must distinguish between:

- A review of a business.
- A review of a neighborhood.
- A report of incorrect data.
- A subjective lifestyle observation.

### 12.5 Future data sources

Possible later integrations include:

- Municipality open data.
- Public transport feeds.
- Parks and public facilities.
- School and health-facility datasets.
- Population and demographic statistics.
- Building and development data.
- Environmental data.
- Public parking data.
- Bicycle infrastructure.
- Internet availability.
- On-duty pharmacy data where legally and technically reliable.

Do not add a data source merely because it is available. Each source must have a clear license, refresh process, reliability score, and user benefit.

## 13. Data Quality and Confidence

Every report should include a Data Confidence Score.

Possible confidence inputs:

- Percentage of required categories with usable data.
- Age of the latest source update.
- Number of duplicate or unresolved records.
- Percentage of businesses with operating hours.
- Percentage of businesses with reliable status.
- Boundary quality.
- Number of first-party confirmations.
- Agreement between independent sources.
- Density of mapped data compared with similar areas.

Suggested confidence labels:

- High confidence.
- Good confidence.
- Limited confidence.
- Experimental.

The platform should say:

> “The score is based on currently available data and may not include every business or recent change.”

It should not say:

> “These are all businesses in this neighborhood.”

---

## 14. Entity Resolution

The same business may appear in multiple data sources.

The system needs a source-independent canonical place record.

Potential matching inputs:

- Normalized business name.
- Coordinates.
- Address.
- Phone number.
- Website domain.
- Category.
- Source place identifier.
- Distance between coordinates.

Matching results should have:

- Automatic-match confidence.
- Manual-review status.
- Merge history.
- Source references.
- Conflict resolution rules.

Never overwrite source data without preserving its origin.

## 15. Suggested Technical Stack

### 15.1 Development workflow

- **Claude Code** for implementation and codebase work.
- **GitHub** for source control, issues, pull requests, and CI.
- Use this file as the project-level `CLAUDE.md` source of truth.
- Keep architecture decisions in version-controlled documentation.

### 15.2 Frontend and application layer

- **Next.js App Router**.
- **TypeScript**.
- **Tailwind CSS**.
- **shadcn/ui** for accessible interface components.
- Responsive web application first.
- Progressive Web App support later.
- Do not build native mobile applications for the first MVP.

### 15.3 Map

Recommended open-data architecture:

- **MapLibre GL JS** for the interactive map.
- A commercial or self-hosted vector-tile provider.
- OpenStreetMap-derived base map and place markers.
- Marker clustering at lower zoom levels.
- Separate map layers for categories, boundaries, and walking areas.

Alternative Google-heavy architecture:

- Use the **Google Maps JavaScript API** if Google Places data must be directly represented on the main map.
- Do not mix map providers and licensed place data without confirming current policy compliance.

### 15.4 Database and backend services

- **Supabase**.
- **PostgreSQL**.
- **PostGIS** for geographic queries.
- Supabase Auth for user accounts.
- Supabase Storage for user-uploaded media and generated report assets.
- Row Level Security for user-owned data.
- Database functions for scoring and geospatial queries where appropriate.

### 15.5 Data ingestion and geospatial processing

Use a separate Python worker for import and data processing.

Suggested tools:

- Python.
- FastAPI for internal worker endpoints if required.
- pandas.
- GeoPandas.
- Shapely.
- PyOsmium, pyrosm, osmium, or osm2pgsql for OSM processing.
- PostgreSQL bulk import.
- Scheduled jobs for refresh and score recomputation.

Prototype flow:

- Overpass API.
- Small GeoJSON neighborhood files.
- Limited OSM category import.

Production flow:

- Turkey OSM PBF extract.
- osm2pgsql or an equivalent importer.
- PostGIS normalization pipeline.
- Incremental updates or scheduled full refreshes.

### 15.6 Search and geocoding

For production, use one of:

- A paid geocoding provider.
- A self-hosted Nominatim instance.
- Google address autocomplete where its licensing model fits the selected architecture.

Do not use the public Nominatim service as an unrestricted production backend.

### 15.7 Walking accessibility

MVP:

- Use straight-line distance bands with clear labels such as “approximately within 800 meters.”
- Do not call this a real ten-minute walk unless actual routing is used.

Later:

- Use a routing engine or API such as openrouteservice, Valhalla, GraphHopper, or pgRouting.
- Generate walking isochrones for five, ten, and fifteen minutes.
- Account for roads, paths, crossings, and barriers.

### 15.8 AI-generated summaries

AI is optional, not the core product.

Possible use:

- Convert structured metrics into a readable neighborhood summary.
- Explain score differences.
- Personalize the report for a lifestyle profile.
- Summarize first-party neighborhood feedback.

Recommended approach:

- Begin with deterministic templates.
- Add a language model only after the scoring model is stable.
- Use the Anthropic API if AI summaries are added.
- Never allow AI to invent businesses, services, boundaries, or safety claims.
- Generate text only from structured facts supplied to the model.
- Cache generated summaries by report-data version.

### 15.9 Hosting and operations

- **Vercel** for the Next.js application.
- **Supabase** for managed database, authentication, and storage.
- **Railway**, **Fly.io**, or a small cloud virtual machine for long-running Python data jobs.
- **Cloudflare R2** or Supabase Storage for large generated files and data exports.
- **Sentry** for error monitoring.
- **PostHog** or another privacy-conscious analytics tool for product analytics.
- **GitHub Actions** for tests and lightweight scheduled tasks.

### 15.10 Testing

- Vitest for unit tests.
- Playwright for end-to-end tests.
- Database tests for scoring functions.
- Fixture datasets for selected Ankara neighborhoods.
- Snapshot tests for scoring outputs.
- Data-pipeline tests for duplicates, invalid coordinates, and missing categories.

## 25. Risks and Mitigations

### Risk: “Google Maps already does this.”

**Mitigation:**  
Focus on neighborhood-level answers, lifestyle profiles, missing-service analysis, comparison, real-estate reports, and explainable scores.

### Risk: Incomplete business data

**Mitigation:**  
Display confidence, combine sources, allow corrections, start with manually reviewed neighborhoods, and avoid completeness claims.

### Risk: Google licensing restrictions

**Mitigation:**  
Use Google only as compliant on-demand enrichment, isolate licensed fields, store only permitted data, show attribution, and verify terms before release.

### Risk: High API costs

**Mitigation:**  
Use OSM as the baseline, limit enrichment to user-visible requests, use field masks, cache only where permitted, set quotas, and monitor cost per report.

### Risk: Incorrect neighborhood boundaries

**Mitigation:**  
Store source and confidence, manually review MVP areas, allow approximate-area labels, and improve boundaries before expansion.

### Risk: Scores appear arbitrary

**Mitigation:**  
Show category inputs, publish scoring principles, explain score changes, version scoring rules, and avoid hidden paid influence.

### Risk: Review manipulation

**Mitigation:**  
Moderation, rate limits, account history, verification levels, anomaly detection, and clear separation of verified and unverified comments.

### Risk: The app becomes too broad

**Mitigation:**  
Keep the MVP focused on home-seeking decisions and daily-life access. Defer unrelated city-guide and social features.

### Risk: AI hallucination

**Mitigation:**  
Generate summaries only from structured facts, require citations to internal data fields, use deterministic templates first, and prohibit unsupported claims.

## 31. Decision Log

Product decisions made after the original specification. Each entry records what was decided, why, and how it reconciles with the principles above.

### 31.1 Neighborhood business list with Google reviews (lazy-loaded)

**Status:** Accepted · **Date:** 2026-07-12

**Decision.** When a user selects a neighborhood, the neighborhood view lists as many businesses as available for that area, and each business can display up to **5 Google reviews** so users can read resident/visitor feedback in the context of the neighborhood. This extends — it does not replace — the neighborhood-scoring focus of the product: the report and scores remain the primary output, and the browsable business list with reviews is a supporting feature within it.

**Rationale.** Users evaluating a neighborhood want to read what people say about its businesses without leaving the report. This adds decision-useful context that pure scores do not convey.

**Loading strategy — lazy / on-demand (chosen over eager).**
- The business list is populated from the **free OpenStreetMap baseline** (name, category, location, distance, score inputs). This renders immediately with no Google cost.
- Google enrichment (current rating + up to 5 review excerpts) is fetched from the **official Google Places API** only when a user **opens or expands a specific business** within the neighborhood view — not for every business on neighborhood load.
- Rejected alternative (eager): pre-fetching reviews for every business when the neighborhood opens would fire a paid Places Details call per business on every load. §25 flags API cost as a top risk; lazy loading delivers the same reading experience at predictable, sustainable cost.

**Compliance guardrails (binding).**
- Reviews come from the official Places API only. **Never scrape** Google Maps or any Google surface (reaffirms §7, §12.3, §28).
- The Places API returns a **maximum of 5 reviews per place**; the product must not attempt to obtain more. "Up to 5" is the technical and policy ceiling.
- Google-derived fields (rating, review count, review excerpts, hours, status) are stored **isolated** from the permanent open-data / first-party record (§12.3), treated as **refreshable licensed enrichment**, and only cached within Google's permitted window. Google **Place IDs** may be stored long-term; other Google content may not be permanently republished.
- Every displayed review shows required **attribution** and a **link back to Google**. Reviews are shown unmodified and unaggregated as Google requires.
- Verify current Google Maps Platform terms before implementation and before any change to how this data is fetched, cached, or displayed (§12.3, §25).

**Consistency check with §7 ("The product is not").** This feature does **not** make the product "a service that scrapes and permanently republishes Google reviews": reviews are API-sourced, isolated, attributed, capped at 5, and not permanently stored. Businesses remain **evidence for neighborhood scoring**; the list-with-reviews is a supporting view, not the product's purpose.

**Cross-references:** §12.3 (Google Places enrichment), §19.3 (Neighborhood report), §19.6 (Business detail), §25 (API-cost risk), §28 (product principles).
